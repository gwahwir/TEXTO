Title: Wildlife, Oceans, and Biodiversity Under Threat

Ocean and marine conservation faces a confluence of pressures — overfishing, plastic pollution, ocean acidification, and warming temperatures — that are transforming marine ecosystems faster than they can adapt. Fisheries management and overfishing remain central challenges in international waters where governance is fragmented and enforcement is weak.

Coral reef protection has taken on new urgency as bleaching events — driven by thermal stress — have damaged or destroyed significant portions of major reef systems worldwide. Coral restoration efforts have shown promise at small scales but face the fundamental challenge that without adequate emissions reductions, restored reefs will face the same thermal stress that damaged the originals.

Endangered species protection has been a subject of ongoing regulatory rollback and reform. The Endangered Species Act remains one of the most powerful conservation tools in American law, but its implementation involves difficult tradeoffs between listed species and economic development interests.

Wildlife protection from illegal trade continues to require international cooperation. CITES enforcement — the treaty governing international trade in endangered species — faces persistent challenges from traffickers who exploit gaps in customs and enforcement capacity.

Invasive species management is a growing challenge as globalisation and climate change enable new species to establish in ecosystems where they have no natural predators. The economic costs of invasive species in agriculture, forestry, and aquatic systems run to billions of dollars annually.

Plastic waste reduction has advanced through a combination of single-use bans, extended producer responsibility schemes, and voluntary corporate commitments. The proliferation of microplastics in the food chain and in human tissue has added urgency to this agenda. Plastic production reduction targets address the source rather than just the waste end of the problem.

Wetlands conservation provides critical ecosystem services — flood control, water filtration, carbon storage, and habitat — yet wetland loss continues despite regulatory protections. Mangrove restoration has received attention as a particularly cost-effective way to restore coastal ecosystem services and sequester carbon simultaneously.

Animal welfare legislation has evolved to address factory farming reform, as consumer awareness of conditions in industrial livestock operations has grown.
