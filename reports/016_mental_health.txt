Title: Mental Health and the American Crisis of Care

The United States is experiencing a mental health care access crisis that predates the pandemic but has been dramatically worsened by it. Rates of depression, anxiety, and suicidality have climbed across demographic groups, while the supply of trained mental health professionals has failed to keep pace with demand.

School-based mental health services have received increased investment in recognition that schools are often the primary point of contact for children experiencing mental health difficulties. School counselor ratios remain far below recommended levels in most districts, however, limiting the practical reach of these investments.

Crisis intervention teams — which pair law enforcement with mental health professionals for calls involving people in psychiatric crisis — have expanded significantly and shown promise in reducing harmful outcomes. The broader question of how to route mental health calls away from armed law enforcement response is actively debated.

Addiction treatment funding remains persistently inadequate relative to the scale of need. Harm reduction policy, including syringe exchange programs and naloxone availability, has gained acceptance in public health circles but remains politically contentious in some communities.

Psychedelic therapy regulation is an emerging area in which preliminary evidence has outpaced the regulatory framework. Clinical trials for psilocybin, MDMA, and ketamine in therapeutic contexts have shown promising results for treatment-resistant depression and PTSD, leading several states to move ahead of federal action.

Veterans mental health funding has been a consistent legislative priority, though the Veterans Affairs system continues to struggle with access and wait times in many areas. Military sexual trauma response has received particular attention following advocacy by survivors and research documenting the inadequacy of existing support.

Dementia care funding faces an enormous scaling challenge as the population ages. Alzheimer's research investment has accelerated, though a curative or disease-modifying treatment remains elusive. The burden of dementia care falls heavily on family caregivers, predominantly women, who often have limited support.

Involuntary psychiatric holds reform has been prompted by recognition that existing frameworks are often poorly calibrated — sometimes too permissive, sometimes too restrictive — and lack adequate follow-up care after release.
