Title: Wildfire, Drought, and Climate Adaptation

Wildfire prevention and management has become one of the most visible frontiers of climate adaptation in the American West. Longer fire seasons, drier conditions, and decades of fire suppression that allowed fuel loads to accumulate have combined to produce fires of unprecedented scale and intensity.

Forest management and thinning through prescribed burns and mechanical treatment is widely supported by fire ecologists as a necessary component of reducing wildfire risk, but implementation has been constrained by funding, workforce capacity, and regulatory requirements. The tension between ecological values and risk management continues to shape policy.

Wildfire community resilience requires attention not just to forest conditions but to the built environment — building codes, defensible space requirements, emergency alert systems, and community evacuation planning. Wildfire community resilience investments have significant co-benefits for insurance markets, which have been retreating from high-risk areas.

Flood insurance reform has been driven by the financial insolvency of the National Flood Insurance Program, which has accumulated tens of billions in debt following major hurricane seasons. Risk-based pricing, which more accurately reflects actual flood risk, creates significant affordability challenges for existing policyholders in high-risk areas.

Drought relief programs have had to adapt to the reality that drought in the American West is less an episodic disaster and more a permanent condition that requires structural rather than emergency responses.

Urban heat island mitigation has received greater policy attention as extreme heat emerges as one of the leading causes of climate-related mortality. Cool pavement and roofing programs, tree canopy programs, and green building standards all contribute to reducing the heat burden in urban areas.

Earthquake preparedness and seismic retrofitting mandates remain politically difficult in jurisdictions where the risk is well-understood but the cost of retrofitting falls on property owners. The consequences of inadequate preparation are potentially catastrophic.

Extreme heat policy has been hindered by the lack of a federal extreme heat safety standard for outdoor workers, leaving protection to a patchwork of state regulations.
