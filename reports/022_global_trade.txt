Title: Global Trade and Economic Interdependence

Trade policy and tariffs have become an increasingly fraught arena as the post-war consensus in favour of trade liberalisation has come under pressure from distributional concerns, national security anxieties, and the strategic implications of economic interdependence with geopolitical rivals.

Foreign direct investment screening has been significantly strengthened through expanded CFIUS review authority, reflecting concerns about the acquisition of sensitive technologies and infrastructure by foreign state-linked entities. The definition of national security has expanded considerably, creating uncertainty for transactions that would previously have faced no regulatory scrutiny.

Export control enforcement has been tightened significantly for advanced semiconductors and related manufacturing equipment, in a deliberate effort to slow the development of cutting-edge military and AI capabilities by strategic competitors.

Supply chain resilience has become a policy priority following pandemic-era disruptions that revealed vulnerabilities in highly concentrated global production networks. Reshoring and friendshoring initiatives aim to reduce dependence on single-source suppliers, particularly for critical goods.

Child labor in global supply chains has received renewed attention as investigations have documented the persistence of forced and child labour in supply chains for goods consumed in wealthy countries. Corporate social responsibility standards and mandatory supply chain due diligence legislation represent different approaches to addressing this problem.

Debt relief for developing nations has returned to the policy agenda as many low-income countries face debt crises exacerbated by the pandemic, rising interest rates, and climate-related shocks. The involvement of new creditors — particularly China — in sovereign debt markets has complicated the traditional restructuring frameworks.

Fair trade certification programmes have expanded consumer awareness of labour and environmental standards in global supply chains, though critics argue that certification systems are subject to capture and may not deliver on their promises to producers.

WTO dispute resolution mechanisms have been effectively paralysed by the blocking of Appellate Body appointments, leaving the global trade governance system without its primary enforcement mechanism.
