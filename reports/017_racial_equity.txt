Title: Racial Equity and the Unfinished Work of Civil Rights

Racial equity initiatives occupy contested terrain in American politics, simultaneously acknowledged as necessary by a broad public consensus and resisted in their specific implementation by organised opposition. The empirical evidence of persistent racial disparities across health, wealth, education, and criminal justice is not seriously disputed; the appropriate policy response is fiercely debated.

Affirmative action in admissions has been effectively ended by a Supreme Court decision that will reshape the demographics of selective higher education institutions for years to come. Affirmative action in government contracting remains on different legal footing and continues in many jurisdictions.

Reparations for slavery have moved from the theoretical to the legislative in several cities and states, with study commissions and pilot programmes exploring different approaches. The breadth of the concept — who qualifies, what form redress should take, how it would be funded — presents formidable practical and political challenges.

Racial equity initiatives in public health have gained attention following documentation of stark disparities in COVID-19 outcomes that echoed patterns across many other health conditions. Environmental justice communities, which bear disproportionate pollution burdens due to historical land use decisions, have become a focal point for both research and advocacy.

Hate crime legislation has been strengthened following periods of increased violence targeting racial, ethnic, and religious minorities. Enforcement, however, depends on reporting by local law enforcement agencies, which varies significantly.

Indigenous rights and sovereignty encompass a wide range of issues from tribal land rights to resource extraction to child welfare. The legal framework of tribal sovereignty creates a complex jurisdictional landscape that has been the subject of sustained litigation and legislative attention.

Disparate impact in housing law has been upheld as a valid theory of discrimination by the Supreme Court, meaning that facially neutral policies that disproportionately harm protected classes can violate fair housing statutes even without demonstrated discriminatory intent.

Wealth-building for underserved communities through homeownership, small business development, and financial inclusion has emerged as a complement to anti-discrimination approaches.
