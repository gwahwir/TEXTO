Title: Campaign Finance and Democratic Governance

Campaign finance reform has been profoundly shaped by a series of Supreme Court decisions that have sharply limited the range of constitutionally permissible regulation. The result is a campaign finance environment in which spending is effectively unlimited, dark money flows through opaque nonprofit structures, and the connection between money and political access is well-documented if difficult to quantify.

Dark money disclosure requirements represent the most widely supported reform measure because they target transparency rather than spending limits, and therefore occupy firmer constitutional ground. Proposals to require disclosure of donors to politically active nonprofits have attracted support across the ideological spectrum, though they face strong opposition from organisations that benefit from the current opacity.

Super PAC regulation has been the subject of sustained reform advocacy since the Citizens United decision, which created the legal framework for unlimited independent expenditures. The coordination rules that are supposed to prevent Super PACs from working directly with campaigns are widely regarded as inadequate and easily circumvented.

Lobbying regulation has seen incremental reform, with disclosure requirements expanded and cooling-off periods extended. Revolving door restrictions — limiting the speed with which government officials can move into lobbying roles related to their prior work — enjoy broad public support but face persistent pressure from the industries most affected.

Congressional term limits remain popular with the public but opposed by most legislators and by constitutional scholars who note that voters already have the power to replace incumbents. The practical effect of term limits, based on state-level experience, is often to shift power from elected officials to lobbyists and staff.

Ethics in government standards have been the subject of repeated reform proposals, particularly regarding financial disclosure requirements for senior officials and the enforcement mechanisms available when violations occur. Whistleblower protection has been strengthened in some contexts but remains inadequate in others, particularly for national security whistleblowers.

Campaign finance reform cannot be fully separated from broader questions about the role of money in democratic governance and the extent to which current arrangements systematically advantage incumbent wealth.
