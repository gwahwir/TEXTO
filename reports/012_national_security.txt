Title: National Security in an Era of Strategic Competition

The return of great power competition has reshaped American national security strategy in ways that are still being absorbed by institutions designed for a post-Cold War world. Diplomatic relations with China have become the organising frame for a wide range of policy decisions spanning trade, technology, military posture, and alliance management.

Semiconductor supply chain security has emerged as one of the most consequential industrial policy debates in decades. The concentration of advanced chip manufacturing in Taiwan has created a vulnerability that both parties have moved aggressively to address through domestic investment incentives and export control enforcement.

Nuclear nonproliferation remains a cornerstone of international security architecture, yet the regime faces mounting pressure from new entrants, the erosion of arms control treaties, and the intersection of civilian nuclear technology with weapons-relevant capabilities. Nuclear energy expansion, proposed as a climate solution, requires careful navigation of these dual-use concerns.

Counterterrorism strategy has evolved significantly since the high-intensity operations of the post-9/11 era. The current approach relies more heavily on partnerships, intelligence sharing, and targeted operations, with greater scrutiny of the civilian harm caused by drone warfare policy.

Cybersecurity policy has become inseparable from national security, as state-sponsored actors conduct persistent operations against government, military, and private sector networks. Critical infrastructure protection requires coordination across sectors with different regulatory environments and risk tolerances.

Foreign agent registration enforcement has received renewed attention as influence operations — using domestic intermediaries to advance foreign interests — have become a standard tool of geopolitical competition.

Relations with Russia have deteriorated sharply following the invasion of Ukraine, with far-reaching consequences for European security, energy markets, and the architecture of international institutions.

Space debris regulation has emerged as a governance challenge as the number of active satellites and debris objects in low Earth orbit grows rapidly, increasing collision risk.
