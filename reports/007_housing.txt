Title: Housing Affordability and the Urban Crisis

The affordable housing crisis has reached acute proportions in major metropolitan areas across the country, driven by a combination of constrained supply, speculative investment, and decades of exclusionary zoning. Tenant protections and rent stabilisation measures have proliferated in response, though housing economists debate their long-term effects on supply.

Homelessness prevention programs have received increased attention and funding following visible increases in unsheltered populations in cities across the country. Supportive housing for chronic homelessness — which combines permanent subsidised housing with wraparound services — has the strongest evidence base of any intervention, yet it remains expensive and politically difficult to site in residential neighbourhoods.

The housing voucher program reform debate centres on chronic underfunding. Only about one in four eligible households receives assistance due to insufficient vouchers, leaving millions on waiting lists that can stretch for years or even decades. Housing voucher program reform advocates argue that universalising the benefit would be transformative.

Zoning reform for equity has gained unusual momentum, with several states passing laws to allow denser housing near transit corridors or to eliminate exclusionary single-family-only zones. These reforms face fierce opposition from existing homeowners protective of neighbourhood character and property values.

Community land trusts have emerged as a promising mechanism for preserving affordability in gentrifying neighbourhoods by removing land from the speculative market. Short-term rental regulation — targeting platforms like Airbnb — has been adopted in many cities as a measure to return housing units to the long-term residential market.

The intersection of affordable housing crisis with mental health care access is particularly pronounced. A significant share of the unsheltered population has serious mental illness or substance use disorders, and inadequate treatment capacity means that the healthcare system and the housing system are both failing the same people.

Gentrification and community displacement remain deeply contested terrain, with communities of colour disproportionately affected by the displacement pressures that accompany neighbourhood investment.
