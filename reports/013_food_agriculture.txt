Title: Food Systems and Agricultural Policy

Agricultural subsidies in the United States primarily benefit large commodity producers, a distribution pattern that has changed little despite periodic calls for reform. The political economy of farm policy — structured around the farm bill, which combines agricultural and nutrition programmes — makes fundamental restructuring extremely difficult.

Food security and nutrition policy encompasses programmes from SNAP benefits expansion to school lunch quality standards to WIC program reform. These safety net programmes reach tens of millions of Americans and have demonstrated strong effects on child development, health outcomes, and economic stability.

Food desert elimination has emerged as both a public health and an equity issue. Neighbourhoods with low supermarket access and high concentrations of fast food outlets face elevated rates of diet-related disease. Community garden funding and urban farming support represent grassroots responses to inadequate formal food system access.

Factory farming reform has attracted growing attention from both animal welfare advocates and public health researchers concerned about antimicrobial resistance strategy implications. The concentration of livestock production creates conditions in which pathogens can spread rapidly and selective pressure for resistance is intense.

Organic food standards have been the subject of ongoing debate about the integrity of certification processes and the extent to which organic practices deliver the environmental and health benefits claimed. Regenerative agriculture incentives represent a newer framework focused on soil health and carbon sequestration that has attracted interest from across the political spectrum.

GMO labeling requirements have been implemented at the federal level through a somewhat convoluted disclosure system that satisfies neither transparency advocates nor biotechnology companies. Gene-edited food regulation presents new challenges as CRISPR and related technologies produce crops that may be difficult to distinguish from conventionally bred varieties.

Food bank funding has grown substantially in recent years, reflecting both increased need and increased philanthropic and government attention. Gleaning and food recovery programs work to redirect surplus food from farms, restaurants, and retailers before it reaches the waste stream.
