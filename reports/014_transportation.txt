Title: Transportation Infrastructure and the Future of Mobility

Infrastructure investment in transportation has seen a significant influx of federal funding in recent years, representing the largest surface transportation investment in generations. The challenge now is translating authorised funding into completed projects, a process complicated by permitting delays, labour shortages, and supply chain constraints.

High-speed rail investment has long been a point of comparison between the United States and peer nations in Europe and Asia. Amtrak funding and expansion has improved the Northeast Corridor's performance, but true high-speed rail outside that corridor remains aspirational. The capital requirements and right-of-way challenges are formidable.

Bridge replacement funding has received particular attention following high-profile bridge failures. The American Society of Civil Engineers estimates that a significant fraction of the national bridge inventory is in poor condition, representing both a safety risk and a drag on economic productivity.

Public transit fare equity has become a more prominent policy question as agencies consider eliminating or substantially reducing fares to improve access and ridership. Bus rapid transit funding and light rail expansion represent different approaches to expanding urban mobility, with tradeoffs in cost, coverage, and ridership potential.

Autonomous vehicle regulation has advanced unevenly, with federal agencies struggling to update frameworks designed for human-operated vehicles. Self-driving car liability raises unresolved questions about insurance, accountability, and the transition period when human and autonomous vehicles share roads.

Electric vehicle incentives have been structured to favour domestic manufacturing, creating friction with trading partners and some uncertainty for consumers. Vehicle emissions standards continue to evolve, with the pace of the transition from internal combustion engines a central point of contention.

Traffic safety and Vision Zero programmes — which set a goal of eliminating traffic fatalities — have been adopted by numerous cities with varying degrees of commitment and success. Distracted driving laws have proliferated but enforcement remains inconsistent.

Congestion pricing in urban centres has generated substantial evidence of effectiveness at managing demand and funding transit improvements.
