Title: Water Infrastructure and the Coming Scarcity

Water rights and scarcity have moved from a regional preoccupation to a national crisis as aquifer depletion, extended drought, and competing demands strain water systems that were designed for a different era. The politics of water allocation are among the most complex in American governance, intertwining federal compacts, state law, tribal claims, and local regulation.

Lead pipe replacement has emerged as an urgent public health priority following the Flint water crisis and subsequent surveys revealing the prevalence of lead service lines across older cities. The health consequences of lead exposure, particularly for children, are irreversible, making this one of the clearest cases for urgent infrastructure investment.

Drinking water quality standards have come under pressure from emerging contaminants — particularly PFAS contamination, a class of synthetic chemicals associated with a range of serious health conditions that are now detectable in water supplies across the country. Regulatory action has been slower than the scientific evidence warrants, in part due to industry opposition.

Groundwater depletion in the High Plains Aquifer system, which underlies much of the agricultural heartland, poses a long-term threat to food production that has no straightforward solution. Irrigation efficiency mandates and market-based water trading have been proposed, but implementation faces political resistance from agricultural communities.

Stormwater management and combined sewer overflow remain significant sources of water quality impairment in older urban areas. When storm drains and sewage systems share infrastructure, heavy rainfall events can overwhelm treatment capacity and release raw sewage into waterways.

Dam removal and river restoration has gained traction as an approach to improving fish passage and river ecology, particularly in the Pacific Northwest where salmon recovery is tied to cultural, economic, and treaty obligations. The removal of aging dams can unlock substantial ecological benefits while also eliminating safety risks.

Desalination plant permitting has accelerated in drought-stricken coastal regions, though energy intensity and brine disposal present environmental challenges that require careful management.
